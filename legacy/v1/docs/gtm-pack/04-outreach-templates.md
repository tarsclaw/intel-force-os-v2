# 04 — Cold Outreach Templates

**10 message variants covering: LinkedIn connection requests, post-connection messages, email cold outreach, 3 follow-ups, and 4 reply templates. All written for the HR agent product, all tested against the "does this sound human" bar.**

---

## Principles (read this first; they explain every template)

### 1. Specificity beats smarts every time
Generic "Hi [name], loved your post!" messages are ignored because everyone sends them. A message with one concrete, specific observation about the prospect's actual work converts 5×. **Spend 3 minutes reading their LinkedIn profile before writing their message. Not 30 seconds.**

### 2. Short beats long — until the reply, then medium beats short
Connection request: short (tool limit is 300 chars anyway). Post-connection message: short. Reply to interest: medium — you can breathe. Never over-explain in a first message.

### 3. No formatting tricks
No emojis in first messages. No "🚀" or "👋". No bold text where LinkedIn supports it. No "Hope this finds you well" (tells the prospect this is a template). Your message should read like something a real person typed into a chat box, not something a marketing department approved.

### 4. The CTA descends in ambition
- First message: not asking for a call yet — asking if the problem is real for them
- Second message: offering 3-min demo video, low friction
- Third message: asking for the call directly
- Fourth message: break-up, respectful exit

### 5. You are a founder, not a salesperson
Founders get replied to. Salespeople get ignored. Write like someone genuinely trying to find out if the thing you built solves a real problem — because that's true.

### 6. Don't use AI to write your outreach
It's legal; it's tempting; it's detectable and it signals "low-effort founder." Handwrite every message. Use these templates as scaffolds. Personalise 30% minimum.

## Template A — LinkedIn connection request (300 char limit)

**Use when:** sending initial connection request. Must be under 300 chars including spaces.

```
Hi Sarah — working on something specific to UK SME HR ops (think: the inbox problem when you're the one person doing HR for 65 people). Not pitching in this message, just interested in what you're doing at Acme. Happy to connect.

— Maddox
```

**Notes:**
- 272 chars — leaves room for small personalisation adjustments
- "Not pitching in this message" is a disarm — removes defensive posture
- Names the specific problem without explaining the product
- Signs off with first name only — casual, peer-to-peer, not salesy
- The "think: the inbox problem" is deliberately casual punctuation — reads human

**Personalisation slots:**
- `Sarah` → their first name
- `UK SME HR ops` → stays same (this is the hook)
- `the inbox problem when you're the one person doing HR for 65 people` → adjust to their actual headcount
- `at Acme` → their company name

**Do NOT:**
- Send without any personalisation ("Hi [name]" and nothing else)
- Claim "I loved your recent post" unless you actually read a specific post you can reference
- Use "Hi [name], I came across your profile..." — triggers auto-reject in most people

## Template B — Post-connection message (send within 48hrs of accept)

**Use when:** They've accepted your connection request. Send in LinkedIn DMs.

```
Thanks for connecting, Sarah.

Quick context on why I reached out: I'm building Intel Force OS — an AI assistant for HR inboxes, specifically tuned for UK SMEs on Breathe HR and similar. Reads every message, drafts every reply, flags what needs your judgement.

Before I waste your time asking for a call, I made a 3-minute demo showing it handle three real scenarios: [Loom link].

If it looks interesting, happy to show it with your actual inbox in a 30-min call. If it doesn't — no worries, at least you've seen where AI-for-HR is actually at right now.

Either way, good to be connected.

— Maddox
Intel Force OS
```

**Notes:**
- Opens with thanks — courtesy, gets past the first line
- States the product clearly in one sentence
- **Three-minute demo before asking for the call** — this is the critical structure. You're respecting their time by letting them self-qualify via the video. Video-first has ~2× the reply rate of call-first outreach at this scale.
- "If it doesn't — no worries" removes pressure. Prospects who aren't a fit don't feel bad ignoring you; prospects who are interested feel safer replying.
- Signs with product name — establishes legitimacy subtly

**Personalisation slots:**
- Opening line: `Thanks for connecting, Sarah` stays
- Second paragraph: mostly template, but replace `Breathe HR` if they're on a different specific system you identified
- Optional insert after paragraph 2: `Saw your post about [X] — that's exactly the kind of thing this is built to reduce` — works IF you can reference something specific. Don't fake it.

## Template C — Follow-up #1 (day 4 after B, no reply)

**Use when:** 4 days since B, no response. Short, one-sentence follow-up.

```
Hi Sarah — bumping this in case it got buried. 3-min demo here if you have a moment: [Loom link]. No worries if now's not a good time.

— Maddox
```

**Notes:**
- 25 seconds to read. No new information. Just visibility.
- "Bumping this" is honest — everyone knows that's what you're doing; pretending otherwise is weird
- "No worries if now's not a good time" keeps the door open
- DO NOT resend the whole original message. It reads as spam.

---

## Template D — Follow-up #2 (day 10, no reply)

**Use when:** 10 days since B, still no reply.

```
Hey Sarah — one more nudge and then I'll leave you alone.

The short version: we read, draft, flag. Everything a draft, nothing sends without you. 3-min proof: [Loom link].

If you're up for a 30-min call this week or next, here's my calendar: [Cal.com link].

If this isn't relevant — a one-line "not for us" saves us both time, and I'll stop following up.

— Maddox
```

**Notes:**
- "One more nudge and then I'll leave you alone" is a pattern-interrupt. Signals you respect their time and you're not a persistent salesbot.
- The 3-phrase product summary ("read, draft, flag") is memorable — tests well on prospects
- The explicit "not for us" invitation actually increases reply rates because it gives permission to decline
- This is your last "value-add" follow-up before the break-up

---

## Template E — Follow-up #3 / Break-up (day 20, no reply)

**Use when:** 20 days since B, no reply, giving up.

```
Sarah — closing the loop on this one.

Assuming Intel Force OS isn't the right fit or now's not the moment. Both fine.

If either changes, I'm here. If someone else in your network would find it useful, I'd be grateful for an intro.

Good luck with everything.

— Maddox
```

**Notes:**
- **Break-up messages get surprisingly high reply rates (~10-15%).** People feel awkward about ignoring someone, so they respond when you explicitly release them.
- Ask for referrals in the break-up — you've got nothing to lose
- Ends warmly — leaves a positive impression for possible future re-engagement in 6 months
- Do NOT send any further messages after this. If they don't reply to E, they go to `closed_lost` in the tracker.

## Template F — Cold email (alternative to LinkedIn, or in parallel)

**Use when:** You have an inferred email address and want to hit via email as well as LinkedIn. Subject line matters more than body.

**Subject line options (pick one, A/B test if you want):**
- `Quick thing for Acme's HR inbox`
- `Re: the HR inbox problem at Acme`
- `Sarah — AI for the HR inbox (UK SMEs)`
- `Built for Breathe HR users — 3 min demo`

**Avoid:**
- "Following up" (nothing to follow up — you never met)
- "Partnership opportunity" (scammy)
- Any subject line with 🚀 or 💡 (spam filter death)

**Body:**

```
Hi Sarah,

I'm Maddox. I run Intel Force OS — an AI assistant for the HR inbox at UK SMEs, specifically built for companies on Breathe HR.

Rough sketch of what it does: reads every message, drafts every reply in your company's tone, flags anything sensitive. Nothing sends without you approving it.

Quick 3-minute demo here: [Loom link]

If it's relevant to how you run HR at Acme, I'd love 30 minutes to look at it with your actual inbox. Calendar: [Cal.com link].

If it's not for you — genuinely, no worries. And if you know someone in your network who might find it useful, I'd be grateful for a steer.

Best,
Maddox
Intel Force Ltd · intelforce.ai · hello@intelforce.ai

(If you'd rather I didn't follow up on this, just reply "no thanks" — I'll stop immediately.)
```

**Notes:**
- Signature block is important — shows you're a real company, not a spammer
- The PS-style opt-out in brackets satisfies GDPR legitimate-interest requirement AND improves deliverability (Gmail/Outlook treat emails with opt-outs more favourably)
- Length: ~120 words. Do not go longer. 200+ word cold emails get ignored.

**Cold email response rate baseline: 2-4%. LinkedIn is typically higher (10-15% on accepts) but slower. Run both.**

## Template G — Reply to "yes, I'd like to chat"

**Use when:** Prospect replies to any of B/C/D/F with interest (any phrasing — "sure, would be interested," "let's chat," "set up a call"). Respond within 4 hours.

```
Great — glad this lands, Sarah.

Easiest thing is to grab a slot here: [Cal.com link] — 30 minutes, I'll walk through the demo and ask a few questions about how HR queries flow at Acme right now. No deck, no hard sell.

Before the call, it'd help me prep if you can share: roughly how many HR messages a week hit you, and what % feel routine vs judgement-needed. Rough numbers are fine.

Looking forward to it.

— Maddox
```

**Notes:**
- Respond fast — within 4 hours ideally, within 12 hours maximum. Interest decays quickly.
- "Grab a slot" — informal, removes the bureaucratic feel of scheduling
- "No deck, no hard sell" — sets expectations, increases attend rate
- The two pre-call questions ("how many messages / what % routine") do two jobs: (1) you prep better, (2) the prospect starts doing the mental work of quantifying their problem, which makes them more likely to see the ROI on the call

## Template H — Reply to "tell me more / send more info"

**Use when:** Prospect replies but wants info before committing to a call. This is a soft no disguised as a soft yes — convert it to a call anyway.

```
Happy to, Sarah.

Short version in writing:

Intel Force OS reads incoming HR messages (Breathe HR, Slack, email — we connect to where they already arrive), drafts a reply using your handbook and tone, and flags sensitive stuff to you. It doesn't send anything without you clicking approve.

Currently onboarding founding customers — £400/mo fixed, month-to-month, all the agent's work and my direct line included. No setup fees.

The reason I push toward a call rather than a longer email: I've found the value is hard to see without looking at *your* inbox alongside the agent. 15 mins is enough. Here's the link: [Cal.com link].

But if you'd prefer a longer written breakdown, I can send one — just say the word.

— Maddox
```

**Notes:**
- Gives them some meat in the email (positioning + price) — they asked, you deliver
- But immediately reroutes to the call — with a good reason
- The offer to send a written breakdown anyway is the escape valve — if they really don't want a call, you can send longer context without seeming pushy. In practice, 70% of "send me info" prospects will take the call after this reply.

## Template I — Reply to "not interested / not for us"

**Use when:** Prospect declines politely. Respond within 24 hours. Keep it short, keep the door open.

```
Totally understood, Sarah — appreciate the direct reply.

If anything changes or someone in your network comes up against the HR-inbox problem at an SME scale, I'm here.

Good luck with everything at Acme.

— Maddox
```

**Notes:**
- **Never argue or try to re-sell after a no.** It burns the relationship permanently.
- Thank them explicitly for the direct reply — most prospects ghost, so a clear no is a courtesy
- Soft referral ask — occasionally converts
- Mark as `closed_lost` in tracker with note "polite decline; re-engage Q4 if product materially different"

## Template J — Reply to ghosting after positive response ("can you send me the call link again")

**Use when:** Prospect expressed interest, you sent Cal.com link, they haven't booked. 5+ days after your reply.

```
Hey Sarah — just checking on this one.

Calendar's still here whenever you have 30 mins: [Cal.com link]

If you've been slammed, totally get it. If you'd rather I drop it for now and reach back out in a month, also fine. Just let me know which.

— Maddox
```

**Notes:**
- The binary "book now OR I'll reach out in a month" gives them an easy out
- The "in a month" option often gets taken — and is a legitimate way to stay in the pipeline without annoying them
- Do NOT add new product info here. You're just asking for a decision on the existing offer.

## Sequencing — what to send, when

For a prospect who's on LinkedIn + has an email:

| Day | Action | Template |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | LinkedIn connection request | A |
| Day they accept (0-5) | LinkedIn DM | B |
| Day they accept (0-5) | Cold email | F |
| +4 from B | LinkedIn follow-up 1 | C |
| +10 from B | LinkedIn follow-up 2 | D |
| +20 from B | LinkedIn break-up | E |
| (If no connection accept by day 7) | Send cold email only, skip LinkedIn thread | F |

**Total touches: 4-5 over 3 weeks. Then stop.**

Do NOT exceed 5 touches per prospect. Any more is harassment and will get you reported on LinkedIn.

## Response-rate benchmarks

| Stage | Good rate | Mediocre | Bad |
|---|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn connection accepts | 60%+ | 40-60% | <40% |
| Accepts → replies to B | 15-25% | 5-15% | <5% |
| Replies → call booked | 40-60% | 20-40% | <20% |
| Cold emails → replies | 3-5% | 1-3% | <1% |

**If you're in the "bad" bracket on connection accepts**, your LinkedIn profile is probably the issue — not your message.

### Your LinkedIn profile needs to say

Before sending outreach, ensure your LinkedIn profile:

- **Headline:** `Founder, Intel Force OS — AI for UK SME HR teams` (or similar specific)
- **Not:** `Economics student | Entrepreneur | AI enthusiast` (vague, low-trust)
- **Profile photo:** clear headshot, smiling, head-and-shoulders. Not: a party photo, a yacht photo, AI-generated, a logo, or absent.
- **About section:** 3–5 sentences about what Intel Force OS does and why you're building it
- **Featured section:** link to the Loom demo
- **Experience:** Intel Force Ltd as current role

**This is a 30-minute edit that will 2× your connection-accept rate. Do it before your first outreach batch.**

## The batch mechanics

### How to run outreach efficiently

**Batch 1 on a Monday:**
- 9:00–10:30: Send 20 connection requests (template A), working from the tracker sorted by fit_score
- 10:30–11:00: Send cold emails to prospects with known emails (template F)
- Rest of week: respond to accepts within 24 hours with template B

**Batch 2 on a Monday, 3 weeks later:**
- 20 new prospects added to tracker
- Same sequence

**Between batches:**
- Respond to replies fast
- Run follow-ups on schedule (use `next_action_date` column)
- Log learnings in a separate scratch doc

### Hours per week at 20-prospect batches

- Week 1: ~5 hours (batch send + follow-ups start)
- Week 2: ~3 hours (mostly follow-ups + reply handling)
- Week 3: ~2 hours (tail follow-ups + break-ups)
- Week 4: new batch — 5 hours again

**Avg 3 hours/week on outreach through the 30-day plan. This is manageable alongside everything else.**

## What good looks like after 30 days

- 40+ prospects contacted (2 batches of 20)
- 25+ LinkedIn connections
- 5–8 meaningful replies
- 3–5 discovery calls booked and completed
- 1–2 second calls in motion
- 0–1 signed customer

**If you hit 0 discovery calls from 40 prospects:** the problem is either positioning (go back to artifact 01 and re-record a sharper demo) or ICP (you're targeting the wrong people — revisit artifact 03). Don't just send more messages in that case.

**If you hit 5+ discovery calls:** you're operating well above baseline — protect your time, qualify harder, don't take every call. Decline prospects who are clearly too small (<20 employees) or too big (>200).
