# Case Study Playbook

**How we produce the first customer case study once Clawd is live with a real customer. Interview framework, metrics to capture, publication structure, legal approvals.**

> **Audience:** the founder producing the first case study; designer laying it out; anyone using the study in future sales conversations.
>
> **Status:** v1.0. Written pre-launch. First real case study is expected ~months 2–4 post-launch once a customer has genuine operating history.
>
> **Why this matters:** a first case study with specifics (real numbers, named customer, concrete workflow) unblocks more pipeline than any marketing copy. It also converts existing pipeline faster. Worth doing carefully.

---

## 1. What a good case study does (and doesn't do)

### 1.1 What it does

- Gives a real-world answer to "does this actually work?"
- Makes the abstract concrete — "AI agents run your ops" → "Meadow Lane Dental's Proposal Builder drafts 8 proposals/week in 4 minutes each"
- Provides evidence for specific buyers: same industry, similar size, similar pain
- Gives the founder a single artifact to point to in sales calls ("here's how this played out for someone in your position")
- Validates to the market that Clawd has real paying customers

### 1.2 What it doesn't do

- Replace discovery calls — case studies support, never substitute for, tailored conversation
- Promise the same results for every customer — every deployment has different specifics
- Create urgency by itself — it's evidence, not pressure

### 1.3 What makes a case study actually useful

**Specific over general.** "Saved 60 hours/month" beats "dramatically improved efficiency."

**Named over anonymous.** "Meadow Lane Dental, Birmingham" beats "a UK dental practice." Anonymous studies are less credible.

**Numbers over adjectives.** Output volume, time saved, revenue impact, cost per output — every case study should have at least 3 specific, defensible numbers.

**Honest about tradeoffs.** "We had to adjust the voice profile 3 times before the brand was right" is more credible than "it was perfect from day one."

**Workflow-centred, not feature-listed.** Show what changed in their day-to-day, not a list of Clawd features they use.

## 2. When to produce the first case study

### 2.1 Readiness criteria

A customer is ready to be a case study when:

- [ ] They've been on Clawd at least 60 days
- [ ] They have measurable output across at least 2 agents
- [ ] They've resolved at least 3 escalations (shows real use, not setup-then-abandoned)
- [ ] They're not currently in payment or support dispute
- [ ] They've expressed spontaneous positive feedback at least once
- [ ] The primary contact has confirmed willingness to be interviewed and publicly associated

60 days is the minimum for meaningful metrics. 90+ days is better. Don't rush to publish with 30-day data — it looks thin.

### 2.2 Selecting who goes first

Ideal first case study customer:

- **Agency tier or Growth tier** — not Starter (story is thinner)
- **Principal is articulate** — sales materials benefit from a quotable human
- **Industry representative** — dental if we have dental customers, marketing agency if we have several
- **Clean use case** — workflow that's easy to explain in one paragraph (e.g., "Fathom call → proposal drafted → human reviews → sent")
- **Willing to share logo + photos** — credibility booster

Ask permission explicitly. No case study is worth publishing without explicit written consent.

### 2.3 What to offer the customer in exchange

- Mutual backlinks (they link to clawd.ai from their site; we link from case study)
- Inclusion in our marketing — their brand gets visibility to our audience
- A month of free service OR founding-customer status (if they're not already)
- First access to new features in beta

Don't pay cash for case studies. Creates bad optics and weakens the study's credibility.

## 3. The interview

### 3.1 Format

- 60-minute structured conversation
- Recorded (with permission) — Fathom handles this; gives a searchable transcript
- In-person if practical; video call otherwise
- Primary contact + one other user if possible (different perspectives strengthen the story)

### 3.2 Before the interview

Prepare:
- Internal review of the customer's Clawd usage: invocations per agent, escalations resolved, top outputs
- Screenshots of their real (anonymised if needed) activity
- A draft of the 5 headline numbers we'd want them to verify
- Specific workflow examples to probe ("your May monthly report for Client X took 23 seconds of agent time — what did that used to look like?")

### 3.3 Interview questions (flexible script)

**Setting the stage (5 min):**
- Tell me about your business. What do you do, who do you do it for?
- What did your operations look like 6 months ago? Be specific — what did a typical week feel like?

**The problem (10 min):**
- What was breaking or painful? Give me specifics — which tasks, which times of week?
- What had you tried before Clawd? Tools, processes, workarounds?
- What was the tipping point that made you look for a solution?

**The evaluation (10 min):**
- What made you consider Clawd specifically? What were you skeptical about?
- What made you decide to try it?
- How did onboarding go? What surprised you — good or bad?

**The current state (20 min):**
- Walk me through a typical week now. What's different from before?
- Tell me about [specific workflow based on agent usage]. What happens? How is it different from before?
- Which agent do you find most useful? Which do you use least?
- Tell me about a time Clawd got something wrong. How did you notice? What happened?
- What's the piece you can't imagine going without?

**Numbers (10 min):**
- Time saved per week (best estimate):
- Output volume increase:
- Quality change (subjective, but record their words):
- Any revenue/commercial impact you can attribute?
- How has this affected the team — headcount, morale, focus?

**Looking forward (5 min):**
- What do you wish Clawd did that it doesn't?
- Would you recommend it? To whom? With what caveats?
- If we published a case study, what would make you comfortable? What wouldn't you want in there?

### 3.4 What to listen for

- Specific phrases they'd use to describe the product — these become pull quotes
- Workflow before/after shifts — these become the narrative spine
- Unexpected uses — features they use in ways we didn't anticipate (valuable for future marketing)
- Friction points — things that went wrong — we include these honestly; case study credibility depends on it

## 4. Metrics to capture

### 4.1 Baseline (before Clawd)

Self-reported; not defensible to the same standard as post-Clawd data. Ask:

- Hours/week spent on [specific workflow]
- Output volume in a typical month
- Team size dedicated to the work
- Cost per output (rough)

### 4.2 Post-Clawd (from Clawd telemetry)

Defensible numbers, pulled from our observability:

- Invocations per month per agent
- Average invocation duration
- Total cost (our side) per month
- Escalation count and resolution rate
- Outputs produced (count)
- Budget utilisation

### 4.3 Impact framing (the moment that sells)

Examples of numerical framing that lands:

- "Proposals went from taking 90 min → 4 min of review + 1 min of edit. 12x faster."
- "Monthly reports for 18 clients, which used to take ~2 days of one analyst, now take 3 hours of review across all of them."
- "Team freed up ~15 hours/week, reallocated to business development."
- "First month's cost: £1,800 subscription + £420 usage = £2,220. Replaced ~40 hours of £40/hr contractor work per month = ~£1,600/month gross, plus capacity for higher-leverage work."

### 4.4 Numbers to AVOID using

- "10x productivity" without specifics — sounds like nothing
- "Revolutionised operations" — never
- Industry-wide stats (e.g., "AI saves 40% of time" — not our number)
- Projections ("will save £500k/year") — only actuals

## 5. Case study structure (the publishable artifact)

### 5.1 Format

- Single-page web document: ~800–1,500 words
- Hosted at `clawd.ai/customers/{customer-slug}`
- Linked from landing page "Customers" nav
- Downloadable PDF version for sales collateral

### 5.2 Section structure

**1. Hero block**
- Customer name + logo
- 1-sentence tagline: what they do
- 3–4 headline numbers (specific, big, credible)
- Quote from primary contact (one line)

**2. Who they are (1 short paragraph)**
- Industry, size, location
- What they do and for whom

**3. The challenge (2–3 short paragraphs)**
- Before state — specific and honest
- What they'd tried
- What wasn't working

**4. What they did with Clawd (2–4 short paragraphs)**
- Which agents they deployed
- How the workflow changed
- What their onboarding was like (honest about bumps)

**5. The outcomes (1 paragraph + numbers)**
- Specific before/after
- The numbers (again, larger, easier to scan)
- A pull-quote from the customer

**6. What's next**
- How they plan to use Clawd in coming months
- Any expansion plans

**7. CTA**
- "Want to see if Clawd could fit your operations? [Book a demo]"

### 5.3 Visual elements

- Customer logo (prominently, top)
- Photos of the customer team (if they'll share) — humanises the case
- Screenshot(s) of their real Clawd dashboard (consent required; may need anonymisation of client names within)
- Simple diagrams of the workflow before vs after — not fancy, just functional
- Pull quotes formatted for scannability

### 5.4 What to AVOID

- Stock photography — always looks fake
- Corporate-brochure language — we're specific, not vague
- Excessive Clawd branding in the case study — the customer is the star
- Hiding drawbacks or friction — credibility collapses if readers sense omission

## 6. Legal clearance

### 6.1 Customer approval flow

1. Draft case study based on interview
2. Send full draft + all quotes to customer's primary contact
3. Get their edits and approval
4. Get approval from their marketing/comms team if they have one
5. Confirm specific claims they'd attest to (numbers, quotes)
6. Get written approval (email is fine) to publish

Never publish until #6 is complete.

### 6.2 Review checklist before publish

- [ ] Customer has seen the final draft
- [ ] Customer has approved quotes attributed to them
- [ ] Customer has approved their logo usage
- [ ] Every number is backed by evidence we can produce on request
- [ ] No customer-of-customer data is disclosed (the customer's own clients' info)
- [ ] Any screenshots of the dashboard don't leak customer-of-customer data
- [ ] Any NDA or confidentiality clauses in the customer's MSA with us are respected
- [ ] Their sub-processors / suppliers aren't revealed if inappropriate

### 6.3 Termination of case study

If the customer leaves or asks to remove the case study:

- Remove from the website immediately
- Archive PDF from sales collateral folder
- Remove from any future-dated campaigns
- Update CRM to note: do not reference

Keep a record (internally) of case studies that were once live. This is both historical and protects us in case of disputes.

## 7. Production timeline (from decision to published)

| Step | Duration |
|---|---|
| Decision to pursue + customer agreement | 1 week |
| Interview prep + interview | 1 week |
| Draft writing | 1–2 weeks |
| Customer review cycle | 1–2 weeks |
| Design + layout | 1 week |
| Legal clearance | 1 week |
| Publish | Day zero |

**Total: ~5–8 weeks.** Budget for longer if the customer is slow to respond.

## 8. After publication

### 8.1 Distribution

- Email existing prospects who are in the same industry / have similar pain
- Share on LinkedIn (founder post with tags to the customer if they consent)
- Include in next month's newsletter (if we have one)
- Link from landing page "Customers" nav (replacing empty placeholder)
- Reference in demos: "Here's a customer in a similar space who..."

### 8.2 Measurement

Track:
- Clicks to the case study page
- Time on page
- Downloads of the PDF
- Inbound leads that cite the case study

Track this for 90 days to see if the case study correlates with conversion.

### 8.3 Retirement

Case studies don't age well. Revisit annually:
- Is the customer still using Clawd? (If no, decide: pull or refresh with current state?)
- Do the numbers still land? (Market shifts; £20k/year saved in 2026 is less impressive in 2028)
- Are there newer, stronger stories? If so, promote the new one; archive the old

## 9. Building a portfolio over time

### 9.1 Diversity matters more than volume

At 10 case studies, we want:
- Different industries represented (not all dentistry; not all marketing agencies)
- Different tiers (at least one Starter, two Growth, one Scale, ideally one Enterprise / Agency Partner)
- Different workflows (not all proposal-drafting; include content, lead research, reporting)
- Different geographies if customers exist (UK primary, but an EU or US customer is valuable for geographic signal)

A portfolio of 10 diverse case studies is more useful than 30 of the same shape.

### 9.2 Cadence

- First case study: months 2–4 after launch
- Second case study: month 6
- Third and fourth: months 9–12

After first 10, drop to 3–4 per year. Focus on depth (richer stories, more visual) over volume.

### 9.3 Different formats for different uses

Once we have enough material:
- **Full case studies** — longform, web + PDF (as above)
- **Customer quotes** — collected on a `customers` page with logos
- **Metric-focused teasers** — "Meadow Lane: 60 hours saved/month" card for social + ad use
- **Video case studies** — 60–90 second customer-on-camera for landing page (expensive; do once we have a customer who'll be on camera)

## 10. Internal tooling for case studies

### 10.1 The case study folder

Each case study gets a folder (e.g., `/marketing/case-studies/meadowlane-dental/`):

- `interview-transcript.md` — full Fathom transcript
- `draft-v1.md`, `draft-v2.md` — iterations
- `final-approved.md` — the published version
- `customer-approval.eml` — record of written consent
- `metrics-backing.csv` — numbers referenced, with sources
- `screenshots/` — (consent-cleared)
- `logo/` — their logo (high-res, with usage permission note)

Kept forever. If someone challenges a claim, we can produce evidence.

### 10.2 Who owns this

Founder initially. A marketing hire (if that ever happens) owns the process at 20+ customers. Until then, it's a deliberate founder activity — at ~2 hours per week of founder time for the first 6 months post-launch.

## 11. Ethical considerations

- Never fabricate numbers — if we can't back a claim, it doesn't go in
- Never attribute quotes without explicit permission
- Never publish about a customer without their full approval
- Don't let the case study misrepresent Clawd's capabilities (e.g., "Clawd handles all our support tickets" if it doesn't — that's a future-feature claim disguised as a testimonial)
- Respect customer confidentiality about their own customers
- When a customer churns, honour their privacy — don't keep dragging their case study around as "still relevant"

## 12. Open decisions

**OD-P5-30:** Offer content production service (we write + design the case study) or self-serve (customer does their own and we publish)?
- **Recommendation:** We produce. Customers are busy. Our version is better positioned. Cost: 6–10 hours of founder/writer time per case study.

**OD-P5-31:** Case study format primary — web page, PDF, or video?
- **Recommendation:** Web page primary, PDF export for sales, video for a subset. Video is too expensive to make the default.

**OD-P5-32:** Include financial figures (revenue increase, £ cost savings) or stick to operational metrics (time saved, volume)?
- **Recommendation:** Include operational metrics by default; include financial figures only when the customer is comfortable and numbers are specific (not projections).

## 13. Templates

### 13.1 First-contact email (to candidate customer)

```
Subject: Would you be up for a short conversation?

Hi {{name}},

You've been using Clawd for {{duration}} now — by my count, {{metric e.g. "you've generated 40+ proposals and 12 client reports"}}.

We're putting together our first case studies to help other agencies see
how Clawd shows up in the real world. I'd love to talk to you about
your experience — both the working parts and where we've gotten things wrong.

If you're open to it: 45 minutes, recorded, I turn it into a case study
draft that you fully approve before publishing. In return, you'd get a
backlink from our site and inclusion in our marketing.

No pressure at all — let me know either way.

{{signature}}
```

### 13.2 Post-interview thank-you

Short email thanking them, confirming next steps, and naming the approval timeline.

### 13.3 Approval request

Send the full draft with every quote highlighted. Explicit ask: "Please mark any edits and reply with 'approved' once you're happy."
