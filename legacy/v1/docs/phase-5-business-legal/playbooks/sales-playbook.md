# Sales Playbook

**How Clawd deals get closed. Qualification criteria, discovery-call structure, demo flow, objection handling, pricing conversations, close tactics.**

> **Audience:** the founder (primary seller for v1); anyone else selling Clawd. The playbook is a living document — update after every 5 deals based on what actually happens.
>
> **Status:** v1.0. Written before the first real deal closes, so much of this is hypothesis until data corrects it.
>
> **Honest note:** this playbook reflects what should work for UK B2B professional services. First customers will test it. Document what actually closes, update accordingly.

---

## 1. Philosophy

### 1.1 We sell to people who already know they have the problem

Clawd is NOT a product you sell via cold outreach to arbitrary agencies. The ICP already knows:
- They have too much repeatable work
- Their team is stretched
- AI tools exist but nothing stitches them together
- They've tried ChatGPT and Zapier but gave up

The sales motion isn't "create demand." It's "identify existing pain and show them a fit."

### 1.2 Consultative, not pushy

- We sell better by understanding the prospect's specific operations than by listing features
- Long-tail close (days-to-weeks) is normal; rushed closes are suspect
- Discounting to close a bad-fit customer costs more than losing them

### 1.3 Qualify out fast

- Bad-fit customers drain support and churn. Qualifying out at the discovery stage is a positive outcome.
- If a prospect's pain doesn't map to our agents, say so — don't contort the product to fit.

## 2. Who we sell to (ICP)

### 2.1 Primary ICP

**UK-based professional services firm or agency:**
- Revenue £500k–£10M
- Team size 5–40
- Principal-led (founder/director involved in day-to-day ops)
- High-volume of repeatable written output (proposals, client reports, content, follow-up emails)
- Already paying for SaaS tools (CRM, call-recording, email marketing)
- Someone on the team has tried AI tools at individual level

**Example ICPs:**
- UK digital marketing agencies serving SME clients
- Dental groups with 3+ practices (multi-site dental or orthodontic)
- Boutique accounting or bookkeeping firms
- IT services consultancies (Rigby-Group-SCC shape)
- Commercial law firms (specific practice areas with repeatable drafting)

### 2.2 Secondary ICP

**Agencies managing multiple brands (agency partner pricing):**
- Holding companies with multiple operating brands
- Agency networks with 3+ client-facing entities
- Marketing agency running multiple client sub-brands

### 2.3 Who we explicitly don't sell to

- **Solo freelancers** — doesn't have the operational complexity; our pricing is wrong for them
- **Enterprise corporates (>1000 people)** — our Starter/Growth/Scale tiers don't match; Enterprise tier is fine but needs a different playbook
- **Startups without product-market fit** — unstable buyers; often churn when they pivot
- **Agencies where the principal isn't engaged** — Clawd onboarding requires someone who knows the brand voice and approves escalations; if the CEO is hands-off, the product won't deliver
- **Industries where AI-output regulation is strict and unclear** (NHS-contract medical services, financial advice to retail consumers without other controls) — we can serve edges of these, but the deal complexity isn't worth the commercial size at MVP

## 3. Qualification framework (BANT-ish)

### 3.1 Four dimensions to qualify on

1. **Budget.** Can they afford £450–£4,500/month?
2. **Authority.** Is our contact the decision-maker or close to it?
3. **Need.** Do they have the pain we solve? Is it top-3 pain?
4. **Timing.** Are they ready to act in next 30–60 days?

### 3.2 Qualification questions (early in discovery)

Early in the first call, work in:

- "Tell me about your operations — what work happens every week that someone groans about?"
- "How many client proposals / reports / pieces of content does your team produce in a typical month?"
- "What have you already tried to help with that?"
- "Who else would be involved in a decision like bringing in a new tool like this?"
- "If you were to move forward, what would that timeline look like?"

### 3.3 Quick-kill criteria

If discovery reveals:
- Their pain is genuinely a single-person task (no scale) — not a fit
- Their CRM is home-grown with no API — integration cost exceeds deal value
- They don't have decision authority and can't name the decision-maker
- They're shopping for the cheapest AI subscription (price-primary buyer) — not a fit
- Their industry has specific regulatory restrictions we haven't figured out — defer

...end the call professionally, offer to reconnect later if circumstances change. Don't push.

### 3.4 Strong-signal criteria

If discovery reveals:
- They describe a specific workflow that one of our agents solves
- They mention a specific dollar/hour cost they'd save
- They're already evaluating other tools (competitive but real intent)
- The person on the call is the founder or head of ops (authority + knowledge)
- They ask about implementation timeline, not just price (buying mode, not browsing)

...aggressive qualification is over; shift into discovery + demo prep.

## 4. Discovery call structure (30–45 min)

### 4.1 Before the call

- Read their website, LinkedIn, any intake form answers
- Identify 2–3 agents most likely relevant to them
- Have a demo tenant ready showing those specific agents

### 4.2 Agenda (stated at the start)

- "Here's what I thought we'd do: 10 minutes understanding your operations, 10 minutes showing you what I think might fit, 10 minutes on next steps. OK?"

Always ask. Always stick to it.

### 4.3 First 10–15 minutes — discovery

Open-ended questions. Let them talk. Take notes verbatim (Fathom-assisted).

Core questions:
- Walk me through what happens when a new client signs with you. What's the first 14 days look like?
- How do proposals get written? Who writes them, from what inputs, how long does it take?
- What gets produced monthly for your clients — reports, content, anything else?
- Where does the team feel stretched? What gets put off?
- How do you keep track of prospects who go cold?

Listen for:
- Specific agent-fits (proposals = Proposal Builder, reports = Reporting Engine, cold follow-up = Follow-Up Pilot)
- Data sources they already use (Fathom = easy; home-grown CRM = hard)
- Dollar/hour cost framings they use naturally

### 4.4 Middle 10–15 minutes — product demo

**Only demo the agents that matched their discovery.** Generic demos are bad demos.

Structure:
1. "Based on what you just said about [specific pain], let me show you [specific agent]."
2. Open the dashboard on the demo tenant
3. Walk through the workflow: trigger → agent runs → output → human review
4. Let them see the actual Brain view with draft files
5. Pause for questions; answer honestly

What to show:
- The Operations Control view with activity
- A recent invocation detail, opening to show output and cost
- An escalation example (show that Clawd stops when uncertain — this is always credibility-building)
- The Brain view with an actual draft proposal / report / article
- The settings panel (they're validating it's real software, not a wireframe)

What NOT to show:
- Every agent (overwhelming)
- The dashboard's admin surface (confusing; wrong context)
- Future/roadmap features — only what exists today

### 4.5 Final 10–15 minutes — next steps

- "Based on what you've seen, which parts feel most like a fit? Which parts feel unclear?"
- Answer clarifying questions
- Get explicit on: what's their decision process, who else needs to see this, what's the timeline
- Propose a next step — usually one of:
  - Trial (Starter or Growth) — we'll set up their tenant, run first invocations together
  - Custom demo with their data — for larger prospects; we build a small proof-of-concept in their voice
  - Introduce to a decision-maker — if the contact isn't the buyer, warm introduction

Send a follow-up email within 2 hours of the call summarising discussion + next step.

## 5. Demo environment

### 5.1 Demo tenant setup

- Persistent tenant named "demo" with realistic but fictional data
- Fake-but-plausible brand: "Meadow Lane Dental" (a fictional UK dental practice)
- Realistic data volumes: 3 months of invocations, ~20 escalations (mixed statuses), budget filled to ~60%
- Runs on a schedule to keep data fresh (daily synthetic activity)

### 5.2 What's NOT in the demo tenant

- Any real customer data (obviously)
- Unreleased features (demos only show shipped features)
- Admin chrome of real operator account (demo logs in as a tenant, not as IntelForce staff)

### 5.3 Backup demo

In case the live demo fails (network issue, Anthropic outage, bug):
- Pre-recorded screen-recording walkthroughs (3 minutes each, per agent)
- Stored in `/vault/demo-assets/` (for our own reference)

## 6. Objection handling

### 6.1 Common objections and responses

**"We could just use ChatGPT with better prompts."**
> Yes — for some tasks. Clawd's edge isn't the AI model; it's the wiring around it. It uses your actual brand voice, pulls in your past work, runs on triggers not prompts, escalates when uncertain, and stays in sync with your tools. A well-prompted ChatGPT is a research assistant. Clawd is an operations layer.

**"How do I know it won't send something embarrassing?"**
> Short answer: it doesn't send anything. Everything Clawd produces is a draft you approve before it goes out. Every agent has validation hooks that catch common errors, and when there's genuine uncertainty it escalates to you instead of guessing. But the final approval is always human. We call it "Everything drafts, nothing sends."

**"The price is high compared to [competitor or AI tool X]."**
> Compared to single-function tools, yes. What we're replacing isn't Jasper or Copy.ai — it's a human doing the operations. At £1,800/month, Clawd is ~5 days of a junior account manager. The question isn't "is this the cheapest AI tool" but "is this cheaper than the next hire we'd have to make."

**"We're not sure our data is safe with AI."**
> Two answers. First: your data stays in our UK-hosted infrastructure. We use Anthropic's API for inference, and their terms don't train on our traffic. Second: every piece of your data is in a tenant isolated from every other customer. Even if we had a bug, row-level security in our database prevents cross-tenant leakage. Here's the DPA summary [share on-screen]. Happy to walk through specifics.

**"We don't have time to set it up."**
> Setup is our problem, not yours. Growth-tier onboarding takes 24–48 hours of our time, about 1 hour of yours — we handle the wizard, you answer brand-voice questions. We built it operator-first specifically because self-serve SaaS setup burns too much goodwill.

**"How do I know you'll still be around in 2 years?"**
> Fair. Here's our honest position: Intel Force is a small UK company building Clawd. We're selling to real customers, not burning VC money. If we do fail, your vault is exportable — every piece of content lives in git-backed markdown files you can open in Obsidian tomorrow. No lock-in. Here's the DR clause in our MSA [show] that spells out what happens if we go under.

**"Can we wait 6 months to see if you're stable?"**
> Of course — but the cost of that wait is 6 months of output you don't have. Our founding-customer programme gives the first 10 buyers 30% off for a year. If you're a fit and the timing works, that's a meaningful discount on top of reducing your risk.

**"We need a feature you don't have."**
> Tell me exactly what? [Listen]. Three possibilities: (1) we could add it fast — then roadmap it explicitly, not vaguely; (2) it's out of scope — be honest, say so, offer workaround if we have one; (3) it's outside our roadmap — same, be honest, let them decide.

### 6.2 Objections that should qualify them out

- "We want an AI that makes all decisions autonomously" — not us; be honest
- "We want to pay £200/month" — wrong tier; probably wrong fit
- "We want customisation on par with in-house development" — enterprise tier only; most prospects asking this aren't at that tier
- "We want guarantees on AI output accuracy" — no vendor delivers this; explain why, move on or let them go

## 7. Pricing conversation

### 7.1 When to introduce pricing

Not on the first minute. Don't avoid it either — if they ask, answer.

Guideline: share pricing after discovery but before demo, if possible. Frames the demo.

"Before I show you the product, here's the pricing so you're not waiting for it: Starter is £450, Growth — which is probably the fit for your size — is £1,800, and Scale is £4,500. Everything's monthly; 2 months free if you commit annually. OK to move on?"

### 7.2 Handling price pushback

- **"That's more than we budgeted."** — "What did you budget? Let me understand what you're comparing to." Then decide: is this a qualifying-out moment (budget off by 5x) or a negotiation moment (20% off)?
- **"Can you do £1,200 instead of £1,800?"** — "Standard price is £1,800. I can offer £1,800 monthly or £18,000 annually (which is effectively £1,500/month). For founding customers we have a 30% discount for 12 months — that gets you to £1,260. Would that work?"
- **"What do you charge for [specific feature]?"** — Don't unbundle. The tiers are the tiers. "Everything's in the tiers — no per-feature charges. That's deliberate so you can use more of it as you grow."
- **"Can we start smaller and scale up?"** — Yes, usually Starter for a month. "Starter is £450 — fewer agents, one user. Most customers outgrow it within 60 days and move to Growth. Happy to start there if you want a smaller step."

### 7.3 Founding-customer programme sales motion

For customers 1–10:

"We're very early. First 10 customers get our founding rate: 30% off for 12 months, grandfathered pricing for the next 12, and a monthly call with me personally for feedback. Seat 1–10 only; after that, standard pricing."

This works because it's:
- Time-limited (only until we hit 10)
- Slot-limited (a real cap)
- Transparent (no fake urgency)
- Positioned as "you help us, we help you"

### 7.4 What NOT to do on pricing

- Never discount without asking for something in return (longer term, annual commitment, case study, referral)
- Never discount below the economic floor in §2 of pricing-spec
- Never promise custom pricing before escalating to founder — deals signed at weird rates create accounting headaches
- Never quote pricing in writing before a verbal conversation — always discuss so you know why they need that price

## 8. Close tactics

### 8.1 The "summary and confirm"

After a good demo + clean Q&A:

"Based on today, it sounds like Clawd fits what you described about [specific pain]. I'd propose we set up a Growth-tier trial starting [date]. That gets you [specific outcomes they mentioned]. Shall I send the agreement?"

Quiet, clear, direct. No "so what do you think?" that invites ambivalence. Propose the next step.

### 8.2 The "trial"

Low-stakes closes for hesitant buyers:

"Let's take away the risk — run a 14-day trial. We'll set up your tenant, do real onboarding, run it against real data. If it doesn't fit your workflow, no harm. If it does, you convert at standard pricing."

Trial requires a credit card at signup. Auto-converts to paid unless cancelled. Stripe handles this.

Trial-to-paid conversion target: 40%+.

### 8.3 Assumptive close

After answering the final objection:

"Sounds like we're aligned. I'll send the MSA and the first invoice. The onboarding wizard takes about 45 minutes of your time; I'll book 30 minutes next Tuesday 10am to walk through it together. Does Tuesday work?"

### 8.4 When they're not ready

Don't push. Agree the next step:

"I hear you're not ready this quarter. What's the trigger that would make you ready? When should I follow up?"

Record the trigger. Follow up on the stated timeline. Don't generic-check-in every 2 weeks.

### 8.5 Lost deals

If they pick a competitor or say no:

- Thank them. Close gracefully.
- Ask: "What tipped it? Anything I should know?"
- Write it down. If the reason is actionable (missing feature, price, wrong positioning), that's product feedback.
- Revisit in 6 months only if the deal was close and the context has clearly changed.

## 9. Sales cycle length expectations

Rough model for UK SME professional services:

| Tier | Avg cycle from first contact |
|---|---|
| Starter | 3–14 days |
| Growth | 14–45 days |
| Scale | 30–90 days |
| Enterprise / Agency Partner | 60–180 days |

Faster than these is great. Slower than these — red flag; re-qualify.

If a deal is open >120 days without movement, close it in the pipeline (let them come back when ready).

## 10. CRM and deal tracking

### 10.1 Minimum viable CRM

For founder-led sales at MVP, a simple setup:

- HubSpot free tier or Pipedrive (cheap) — don't over-invest in CRM tooling until the pipeline demands it
- Deal stages: Discovery → Demo Booked → Demo Done → Proposal → Trial → Closed-Won / Closed-Lost
- Per-deal fields: company, contact, contact role, tier discussed, ICP fit, objections noted, next step, next-step date
- Notes from every call (Fathom transcripts + written summary)

### 10.2 What to track

- Pipeline value (sum of open deals × probability)
- Average time-in-stage
- Win rate per source (referral vs inbound vs cold)
- Average deal size per tier
- Source of first-touch (Twitter, LinkedIn, podcast, referral)

Revisit weekly in 1:1 review. The hypothesis for the first 10 deals is: referrals convert highest, LinkedIn warm outbound is second, cold outbound barely works. Revise after seeing 10 deals.

## 11. Who the founder hands off to (and when)

### 11.1 MVP — founder does everything

Discovery, demo, negotiation, close, onboarding — founder only. This ensures:
- Voice and positioning stay coherent
- Founder sees real feedback
- Customer gets an actual founder relationship (differentiator)

### 11.2 First hire (after ~10 customers)

Once volume exceeds founder's capacity, first non-founder hire is:
- Customer success manager (NOT a salesperson) — owns onboarding + existing-customer relationship
- Founder stays on sales

Only once volume exceeds founder + CSM combined capacity:
- First salesperson (likely mid-market sales experience, not junior SDR)

### 11.3 Never (yet)

- Outbound SDR hiring — not our motion at this stage
- Commission-heavy comp — creates bad incentives at startup-size deals

## 14. Red flags in deals

Mid-deal signals to pay attention to:

- **Silence after demo** — they're ghosting; follow up once, then deprioritise
- **Request for unlimited free trial** — not ready; likely not serious
- **Multiple stakeholders added after discovery** — legitimate; slows cycle but higher-quality close
- **Legal review before commercial agreement** — proceduralist buyer; longer cycle, better discipline
- **Asking for references immediately** — either smart due-diligence or stalling; find out which
- **Promising "we'll buy after our round closes"** — they're not buying; re-qualify or archive
- **"Our CEO has to approve anything over £X"** — good, surface them now

## 15. Post-close follow-through

### 15.1 Day 1 after signing

- Welcome email with onboarding checklist
- Schedule kick-off call within 5 business days
- Confirm who at the customer will run the Wizard

### 15.2 First week

- Wizard completed
- Integrations connected (OAuth done)
- Voice profile extracted
- First invocations run

### 15.3 First month

- Weekly check-in call
- Resolve any friction early
- Collect early feedback for case study later (per `case-study-playbook.md`)

### 15.4 Handoff to CSM (when we have one)

At month 2, hand off to CSM if the customer is stable. Founder stays available for executive-level calls but day-to-day is CSM-owned.
